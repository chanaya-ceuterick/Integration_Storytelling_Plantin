Who is plantin?

Christophe Plantin (c. 1520-1589) was a French-born printer who founded the influential Plantin Press in Antwerp in 1555. Known for his high-quality books, he produced religious, scientific, and humanist works,including the famous Polyglot Bible.

His dedication to excellence helped spread Renaissance knowledge, and his workshop is now the Plantin-Moretus Museum, a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Civil Rights

Plantin had sworn citizenship in 1550, securing his right to reside and work. As a printer, this was crucial, as a 1546 decree by Charles V required all printers in the Netherlands to be sworn citizens.

Language and Education

There were limited resources, such as grammar books or dictionaries, to learn Dutch. Recognizing this gap, Plantin published French-Dutch conversation guides for Antwerp's immigrant population.

His ambition grew, leading him to collaborate with Cornelis Kiliaan on the first multilingual Dutch dictionary, Etymologicum Teutonicae linguae (1573).

This work included Dutch words with French and Latin translations, focusing on their meanings and origins, and marked the start of modern lexicography. The dictionary was innovative in its use of distinct fonts for each language: Gothic for Dutch, Roman for French, and italic for Latin.

The Role of the Women in Plantin's Business

The lace workers, largely unskilled and unprotected by guild regulations, had no formal education. Within traditional crafts, guilds ensured that labor was performed at an agreed-upon rate, with a maximum of five fully trained journeymen working within regulated hours. Guilds also controlled product pricing, ensuring market conformity, including that of luxury lace accessories.

However, unlike their male counterparts, the women working with lace had no such protections. Many were outside guild supervision, leading to exploitation by entrepreneurs who sought cheap, unskilled labor. In this unregulated environment, workers were paid as little as possible for their labor.

The International Trade in Lace

Thanks to his connections with Gassen, Plantin became the primary importer of lace to the French aristocracy. In 1574, Martine provided the French court with lace collars decorated with royal lilies. The international trade in luxury lace products proved highly lucrative, contributing to the wealth of linen traders, who were among the richest merchants in Antwerp.

Martine Plantin

Martina Plantin (1550–1616) was a notable Flemish lace merchant and printer. From the age of five, she was involved in her father Christophe Plantin’s printing business, and at seventeen, she managed the family’s lace shop.

After the deaths of her father and husband, Jan Moretus, she became the head of the Plantin-Moretus printing business from 1610 to 1614, with daily operations managed by her sons Balthasar and Jan. She is recognized as a formidable businesswoman who significantly contributed to the family’s printing dynasty.

Margaretha Plantin

Margaretha Plantin was instrumental in expanding the Plantin printing business to Leiden, managing finances, logistics, and collaboration alongside her husband, Franciscus Raphelengius. She maintained strong ties with the Antwerp headquarters, ensuring smooth operations and contributing to the family’s legacy in the Northern Netherlands.

Catharina Plantin

Catharina Plantin (1553–1622) was the daughter of the renowned Antwerp printer Christophe Plantin. In June 1571, she married Jean Gassen, a Parisian cloth and lace merchant. Following his death, Catharina returned to Antwerp. While specific details of her contributions to the family business are limited, her return to Antwerp suggests she may have been involved in supporting the family’s printing operations.

Magdalena Plantin

Magdalena Plantin played a key role in the Plantin family’s printing business by supporting its Paris branch. After marrying Gilles Beys in 1572, she helped manage and maintain the branch, which her husband operated.

Magdalena’s efforts contributed to extending the influence of the Plantin printing empire in France, ensuring the family’s operations remained connected and successful across different regions. Her legacy continued through her son, who carried on the Paris branch into the next generation.

Henriette Plantin

Henriette Plantin, also known as Henrica or Henrie, was the fifth and youngest daughter of the renowned Antwerp printer Christophe Plantin. Born around 1560, she was eight or nine years old at the time of her father’s death in 1589.

Due to her young age and the limited historical records, there is no substantial information about her direct involvement in the family printing business. It’s likely that she did not play a significant role in the operations of the Officina Plantiniana.

A family bussiness

Plantin was determined to keep his business in the family as much as possible. Along with his daughters, he trusted his brother-in-law, Pierre Poiret, with significant responsibilities in his business, including managing delicate financial transactions and acting as a middleman in Paris for his import and export of French print and typographical equipment.

Plantin's daughter Catherine was also involved in the business from a young age. She was instrumental in dealing with one of his largest Parisian clients, Pierre Gassen, and was responsible for managing financial transactions. Catherine also demonstrated a keen business sense, negotiating with lace workers and paying suppliers.
